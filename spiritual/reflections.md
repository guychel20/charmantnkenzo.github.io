# Reflections

During a recent temple reflection, I contemplated how covenant identity reshapes professional priorities. The temple's emphasis on order, purpose, and covenants magnifies the idea that work is not only a means to a material end but an arena for discipleship. In the quiet of reflection, I considered the ways my daily decisions - who I mentor, how strictly I enforce security policies, and whether I document corrections - are expressions of covenant commitments. Elder Bednar's rope analogy came to the forefront: when spiritual practices, professional responsibilities, and personal growth are braided together with Christ at the center, they produce resilience and clarity.

This reflection highlighted specific changes in my behavior. I recognized a tendency to prioritize rapid fixes during high workload periods; the temple reflection shifted my default to consider the vulnerable and the long-term impact first. I now intentionally document decisions and escalate when necessary, not to deflect blame, but to preserve institutional trust and protect stakeholders. Furthermore, temple covenants reinforced the importance of silent, steady service, mentoring a junior technician quietly or updating documentation without fanfare, because meaningful impact is often unseen yet durable. The exercise produced concrete actions: scheduling weekly documentation time, instituting a short checklist for any configuration change, and committing to at least one mentoring session per week. The reflection made clear that my professional development is not divorced from spiritual growth; it is an extension of it. When my work echoes covenant identity, prioritizing vulnerability, transparency, and service, the work feels integrated, intentional, and peaceful.
